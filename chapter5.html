The Appreciative Inquiry Framework

Key Point

The Appreciative Inquiry Framework is made up of four elements: Discover, Dream, Design, and Destiny. The Discover phase allows for an appreciation and understanding of self; the Dream phase allows us to create a vision for the future; the Design phase allows us to put our practices into place; and the Destiny phases allows us to exert ownership in our practice.

Activity: Applying the Appreciative Inquiry Framework

Let's further apply the Appreciative Inquiry Framework to our own lives.

Grab your journal or a notebook and go through these four quick steps — one for each stage of the AI cycle:

Discover:

Think of a recent moment when you felt proud, energized, or truly at your best.

Write down: What were you doing? What strengths were you using?

Dream:

Now imagine a future where you live and work like that more often.

Write down: What would your ideal day look like if that "best self" showed up regularly?

Design:

What small habit or change could you introduce to move toward that dream?

Write down: One action you can take this week.

Destiny:

What's one way you'll keep this momentum going?

Write down: How will you remind yourself to keep growing in that direction? (e.g., a sticky note, a calendar reminder, a mantra)

The PERMA Model

Key Point

The PERMA Theory of Well-Being, created by Dr. Martin Seligman, defines key elements that enable flourishing: Positive emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Accomplishment. By building strategies that allow us to promote PERMA in our daily lives, we can take further ownership over our flourishing.

Activity: Applying the PERMA Model

Let's work to further integrate PERMA in our daily lives.

Take your journal or notebook and go through the following questions around each of the elements of the PERMA framework.

Positive Emotions:

Can you describe the best thing that happened to you yesterday?

Engagement:

Can you share a strength that characterizes you and how you use it in your life?

Relationships:

Can you name one person that makes your life better?

Meaning:

Can you name a cause that inspires you?

Accomplishment:

Can you describe one thing that took you out of your comfort zone but you did anyway?

Glossary

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)
A mindfulness-based behavioral therapy that encourages individuals to accept their thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them, and to commit to actions that align with their values.
Appreciative Inquiry
A strengths-based approach to organizational change that focuses on identifying what is working well, and building on those strengths to create a positive vision for the future.
Authenticity
The quality of being genuine and true to oneself, aligning one's actions with one's beliefs and values.
Autonomy
The capacity to make one's own choices and decisions, and to act independently.
Behavioral Activation
A therapy technique used to treat depression, which involves increasing engagement in activities that are enjoyable or meaningful, in order to improve mood and reduce symptoms of depression.
Cognitive Restructuring
A therapeutic technique used to identify and change negative or distorted thinking patterns.
Compassion
A feeling of empathy and concern for others, often accompanied by a desire to alleviate their suffering.
Confirmation Bias
The tendency to seek out and interpret information that confirms one's existing beliefs, while ignoring or downplaying information that contradicts them.
Conscientiousness
A personality trait characterized by being organized, responsible, and diligent.
Constructive Criticism
Feedback that is intended to help someone improve, by pointing out areas for improvement and offering suggestions for how to do so.
Core Values
Fundamental beliefs or principles that guide one's behavior and decision-making.
Critical Thinking
The ability to analyze information objectively and make reasoned judgments.
Cultural Humility
An approach to interacting with people from different cultures that emphasizes respect, openness, and a willingness to learn.
Deliberate Practice
A structured approach to skill development that involves setting specific goals, seeking feedback, and focusing on areas for improvement.
Digital Detox
A period of time during which a person abstains from using digital devices such as smartphones, computers, and social media.
Digital Well-being
The impact of technologies and digital services on people’s physical, psychological, and social well-being.
Empathy
The ability to understand and share the feelings of another person.
Emotional Intelligence
The ability to understand and manage one's own emotions, as well as the emotions of others.
Ethical Frameworks
Sets of principles or guidelines that help individuals make decisions based on moral values and duties.
Executive Function
A set of cognitive processes that are responsible for planning, organizing, and regulating behavior.
External Motivation
Motivation that comes from outside oneself, such as rewards or punishments.
Fixed Mindset
The belief that one's abilities and intelligence are fixed traits that cannot be changed.
Flow State
A state of deep immersion and enjoyment in an activity, characterized by a sense of focus, control, and loss of self-consciousness.
Gratitude
A feeling of thankfulness and appreciation for the good things in one's life.
Growth Mindset
The belief that one's abilities and intelligence can be developed through effort and learning.
Hedonic Adaptation
The tendency for people to return to a relatively stable level of happiness despite major positive or negative events or life changes.
Human Flourishing
A state of optimal well-being, characterized by positive emotions, engagement, relationships, meaning, and accomplishment.
Imposter Syndrome
The feeling of being a fraud or phony, despite evidence of one's competence and accomplishments.
Information Overload
The state of being overwhelmed by the amount of information one is exposed to.
Interoception
The ability to perceive and understand the internal state of one's body.
Internal Motivation
Motivation that comes from within oneself, such as a sense of purpose or enjoyment.
Intrinsic Motivation
Engaging in an activity for the inherent satisfaction and enjoyment it provides.
Learned Helplessness
A state of hopelessness and resignation that results from repeated exposure to uncontrollable negative events.
Mindfulness
A mental state achieved by focusing one's awareness on the present moment, while calmly acknowledging and accepting one's feelings, thoughts, and bodily sensations.
Moral Courage
The willingness to stand up for one's beliefs, even in the face of opposition or adversity.
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life.
Optimism Bias
The tendency to overestimate the likelihood of positive events and underestimate the likelihood of negative events.
PERMA Model
A framework for understanding and promoting well-being, based on five key elements: positive emotion, engagement, relationships, meaning, and accomplishment.
Prosocial Behavior
Voluntary behavior intended to benefit others.
Resilience
The ability to bounce back from adversity and cope with stress.
Self-compassion
Treating oneself with kindness, care, and understanding, especially in the face of failure or suffering.
Self-efficacy
The belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations or accomplish a task.
Self-regulation
The ability to control one's emotions, thoughts, and behaviors.
Social Support
The perception and actuality that one is cared for, has assistance available from other people, and most popularly, that one is part of a supportive social network.
Stoicism
An ancient Greek philosophy that emphasizes self-control, reason, and acceptance of fate.
Values Clarification
The process of identifying and prioritizing one's core values.
Willpower
The ability to resist short-term temptations in order to achieve long-term goals.
